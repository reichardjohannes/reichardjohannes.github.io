Work / ENERCON / Customer Portal
Case Study · 2025 – 2026

Redesigning the operator portal for wind‑park monitoring & service.

ENERCON, one of the world's leading wind‑turbine manufacturers, operated fragmented, siloed tools for monitoring, service orders and spare parts. We unified them into one portal with a single sign‑on entry point, designed across desktop, tablet and mobile.

01 · Problem Space

The Challenge

Pain Point 01

Fragmented tool landscape

SIP, ETC and eShop existed as separate access points with no shared context. Operators switched between systems just to build a complete picture of their portfolio.

Pain Point 02

No central monitoring

Performance data, fault alerts and service orders lived in different places. Issues were noticed late, or only after contact with the service team, never proactively.

Pain Point 03

Manual status tracking

Service orders were tracked by email and phone. No structured overview showed status, responsibility and progress in one place across devices.

05 · Key Design Decisions

Three decisions that made the difference

Each of these was contested at some point. Here's the reasoning behind every choice, and which alternatives were ruled out, and why.

Decision 01

Map as a tab state, not a separate page

Problem

An earlier version had the map as a standalone nav item. Clicking a park pin on the map lost the list context entirely.

Decision

The map is a tab state within Wind Parks. Same dataset, different visualisation; the park list stays visible below it.

Decision 02

Notifications in the dashboard, not an overlay

Problem

A notification-bell icon was consistently overlooked. Operators didn't realise critical alerts were waiting.

Decision

Notifications as a fixed block in the dashboard. Always visible on first load — no click required to see what needs attention.

Decision 03

Consistent card pattern at every level

Problem

Dashboard, park overview and turbine detail looked completely different. Users had to re-learn the interface at every level.

Decision

Donut + sparkline + status dot as one card pattern at every hierarchy level. The mental model is learned once, applies everywhere.

08 · Primary User Flow

The critical path

The most frequent task: an operator opens the portal in the morning, spots a fault notification and traces it through to the relevant service order — on any device, in four clicks.

What makes this flow work
  • The fault is colour-coded on step one, no hunting required
  • Tapping a notification jumps directly to the affected turbine
  • Service Orders lives at turbine level, not park level. Orders are turbine-specific
  • The entire flow takes at most four clicks on all three platforms
Alternatives tested & ruled out
  • Service orders only at portfolio level. Too far removed from turbine context
  • Direct link from notification to order list. Skipped important performance data
  • Fault alert as a modal overlay. Blocked context; operators wanted to keep navigating
  • Notification bell instead of a card. Consistently overlooked in testing
09 · Reflections

What I'd carry forward

What worked

  • Insight-traceable decisions: Every UI choice mapped back to a research quote. In reviews, "why is this here?" always had an answer that wasn't taste.
  • One card pattern, every level: Donut + sparkline + status dot learned once and reused at portfolio, park and turbine level kept the MVP fast to build and easy to learn.
  • SSO framed as content, not nav: Treating SIP / ETC / eShop as a "My Apps" launcher resolved the internal-vs-external confusion that earlier versions kept hitting.

What I'd change

  • Map view earlier: The multi-park operator's map need surfaced mid-research. Bringing it into IA from day one would have saved a restructuring pass.
  • Define the post-MVP backlog up front: BESS Value Modelling and Training Center were scoped late. A clearer phase-2 line would have sharpened the MVP cut.
  • Instrument the critical path: The four-click flow is validated qualitatively; baseline analytics from launch would let phase 2 prove it quantitatively.
