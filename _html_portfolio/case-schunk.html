Work / FANUC / Portal & eShop
Case Study · 2023 – 2024

Portal & eShop — one place for every customer need.

A globally leading industrial robotics manufacturer operated eShop, machine fleet, maintenance and benefits across four disconnected systems. We replaced them all with a single integrated portal — designed end‑to‑end, from the first stakeholder workshop through Scrum delivery.

Challenge

No portal. Four isolated systems

Customers managed eShop, machine registry, maintenance history and service benefits across four separate frontends. No shared identity, no shared navigation, no single sign-on.

My role

Full ownership, Phase 0 to launch

Responsible for all UX deliverables: client communication, stakeholder workshops, research, wireframes, usability testing, Hi-Fi, Dev-Handoff and Scrum-embedded quality assurance.

Outcome

One sign-on, one portal. Live

A fully integrated portal for eShop, machine management, maintenance and benefits. All originally defined vision components delivered. High user acceptance across all three target groups.

/01 · Expert Review

Audit the status quo before touching a single pixel.

Heuristic evaluation of the existing eShop using Nielsen's 10 Heuristics, adapted for B2B and industrial-workflow context. Every issue was documented with a screenshot, a severity rating and a rationale.

23 critical usability problems surfaced. Categorised into Critical, Major, Minor. The resulting prioritisation matrix became the evidence base every later design decision argued from.

/02 · Research & Workshops

Understand who the users actually are.

Two half-day on-site stakeholder workshops with PO & PL defined project goals, scope and success criteria. From there, three user personas were developed: The Buyer, The Machine Admin, The Service Technician.

The journey maps revealed a critical gap: Service Technicians thought in physical machines and serial numbers, not order IDs. The entire legacy backend had it the other way around.

/03 · Wireframes

Structure before aesthetics.

Three full iteration cycles in Balsamiq, from quick Lo-Fi sketches to annotated Mid-Fi wireframes. Each cycle reviewed with PO and PL before the next started. Held the line against pressure to skip to Hi-Fi.

Key structural decisions: Single Entry Point dashboard, clear navigation split between eShop context and machine context, Progressive Disclosure for complex machine data.

Balsamiq Lo-Fi → Mid-Fi 3 Review Cycles V3 Approved
Artefact · Wireframes V1 / V2 / V3 · Annotations · Protocols
/04 · Usability Testing

Validate with real users on real wireframes.

Moderated usability tests with representatives of all three target groups, conducted on Mid-Fi wireframes, not mockups. Scenarios were grounded in the most frequent user tasks per persona.

Findings fed directly into the next iteration cycle. The maintenance overview flow required a dedicated third iteration before it cleared the completion threshold. No screen reached Hi-Fi without a passing test as evidence.

/05 · Hi-Fi & Dev-Handoff

From validated wireframe to pixel-precise delivery.

Final design in Sketch. A full component library was built alongside. Every element specified, every interaction described. Multiple client review rounds before sign-off.

Dev-Handoff included specifications, interaction descriptions and component documentation. Embedded in the Scrum team from day one of development — every standup, every refinement, every sprint review.

/06 · Scrum & Launch

Embedded through every sprint, every review.

Implementation in 2-week Scrum sprints with an interdisciplinary team of internal and external developers. Continuous design QA on staging. Every sprint, not just at the end.

Sprint reviews with the client kept feedback loops short. All originally defined vision components were delivered in scope. Final project acceptance by the client.

/06 · Reflections

What I'd carry forward.

Notes to self
for the next platform

What worked

  • Research before wireframes: The persona + journey-map phase surfaced the machine/order mismatch early. If that had surfaced in Hi-Fi, rework would have been significant.
  • Lo-fi testing: Holding the line against "let's just see Hi-Fi" kept iterations cheap and changes honest. Every failed test on a wireframe was a saved sprint of rework.
  • Embedded delivery: Being present in every standup paid off by sprint two. Design decisions made in the room are better than decisions made in a comment thread.

What I'd change

  • Co-design with dev earlier: We started pairing in week 14. Platform and SAP constraints I learned then should have been inputs to the wireframe phase, not corrections in sprint.
  • Quantitative baseline before launch: The qualitative outcome is clear, but instrumentation on the legacy system was too patchy for a clean before/after number. Better baseline tracking from day one next time.
  • Earlier iteration on maintenance overview: This flow needed the most testing cycles. In retrospect it should have been treated as the highest-risk flow from the Research phase onward.
Related Project · Spin-off

Machine Fleet Configuration for Volvo.

Volvo operates entire FANUC machine fleets for the production of complete vehicles. For optimal use of these systems, FANUC needed dedicated configuration software, developed within the existing portal and closely aligned with its design language.

The software allows Volvo employees to select machine units, define F-Pack data, assemble configurations step by step and submit them internally for approval, seamlessly integrated into the familiar portal workflow.
